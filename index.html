The Business & History of Popular Music in America

Explore how the history of American popular music is deeply tied to the evolution of its business—from the earliest record companies and radio royalties to streaming, lawsuits, and independent artists.

1900s–1920s: Birth of the Industry—Jazz, Copyright & Radio
During the 1920s, the American music industry was transformed by the growth of jazz and the widespread adoption of radio and phonographs. For the first time, music could reach a national audience, creating both new artistic movements and new business empires. Record labels like Victor and Columbia rose to prominence, and the creation of ASCAP in 1914 established a system to ensure songwriters and publishers were paid for airplay. Battles over royalties and copyright shaped the legal framework of the industry, as radio stations and publishers fought over who should profit from the music that defined a generation.
1950s: Record Labels, Payola, & Teen Consumers
The postwar years saw rock ‘n’ roll take over the charts, fueled by an explosion of record sales and a new market: teenagers. Large companies like RCA and Columbia, as well as independent labels like Sun Records, competed fiercely for talent and radio play. In this era, record labels controlled almost every aspect of an artist’s career, from song rights to distribution. The infamous “payola” scandal, where DJs were bribed to play certain songs, revealed how tightly business interests and music popularity were linked. Laws passed in response to the scandal would change the relationship between media and the music industry for decades.
1960s–1970s: Motown, Integration, & the Album Era
The 1960s and 1970s marked a new era in both music and business. Motown Records, founded by Berry Gordy, broke racial barriers and perfected a system where the label controlled everything from songwriting to promotion—ushering in the age of the “hit factory.” Meanwhile, the rise of the album as both an artistic and commercial product changed how music was marketed and sold. The expansion of copyright law to cover sound recordings in 1971 gave record companies—and not just songwriters—more legal power, cementing their role in the business structure of American music history.
1980s: MTV, Superstars, & Major Label Consolidation
In the 1980s, the music business was revolutionized by the arrival of MTV, which made image, branding, and music videos essential for commercial success. Major record labels consolidated into a few powerhouse corporations, and the introduction of the CD brought unprecedented profits. Artists like Michael Jackson and Madonna became global icons, with massive promotional budgets and merchandising empires. The business of music became as much about visual storytelling and marketing as it was about the music itself, reflecting broader trends in American consumer culture and corporate consolidation.
1990s: CD Boom, Hip-Hop, & the Indie Revolution
The 1990s were the most profitable decade in American music history, with CD sales reaching all-time highs and MTV driving pop culture. Hip-hop surged from the underground to the mainstream, and for the first time, artists like Tupac and entrepreneurs like Russell Simmons built their own labels and brands. However, the industry’s focus on physical sales and reluctance to embrace digital technology set the stage for disruption. Indie labels gained ground using alternative business models, and the groundwork was laid for the digital revolution to come.
2000s: Digital Disruption, Lawsuits, & New Models
The 21st century began with a crisis: file sharing and piracy decimated music sales, forcing the industry into a period of lawsuits and innovation. Lawsuits against users and tech companies could not stop the collapse of the traditional business model. The launch of the iTunes Store in 2003 introduced legal digital sales, but at a lower profit margin than CDs. Labels turned to “360 deals,” taking a share of artists’ touring and merchandising as well. For the first time in history, technology companies—not record companies—began to control how Americans accessed music.
2010s–Now: Streaming, Social Media, & a New Industry
The rise of streaming services like Spotify and Apple Music marked a new era in music business and history. The “ownership” model vanished as listeners flocked to monthly subscriptions, and nearly all revenue now flows through just a few platforms. While major labels adapted by cutting deals with tech companies, independent artists found new opportunities through social media and direct distribution. The industry now faces debates over fair compensation and copyright in the digital age, while the democratization of music production and release is reshaping the history of popular music itself.
